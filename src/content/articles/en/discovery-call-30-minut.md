Ever run a 90-minute discovery call? The client left confused, and you forgot to ask about budget. Two weeks of emailing later, you find out the project is 3× bigger than you estimated.

I cut discovery down to 30 minutes and 8 questions. It works better. The client has no time to wander, and I have no time to pitch. Here's the format.

## Before the call: 5 minutes of prep

- A quick look at the client's website (or the competition, if they don't have one yet)
- LinkedIn — how many people are at the company, what sector
- Google: company name + "PR" / "interview" / "case study" — what's been written about them

You'll **never** use any of this to pitch. You use it to ask better questions. If I see a company has 8 people and €2M in revenue, I'm not going to ask whether they have a "marketing team" — I already know they don't.

## The 8 questions (30 minutes)

### 1. "What should the site deliver in 6 months? In a single metric."

The most important question. It forces the client to name a concrete thing, not "be modern and professional."

Good answers:
- "Generate 50 leads a month for our sales team."
- "Raise conversion from 1.2% to 2%."
- "Cut support tickets by 30% through self-service documentation."

Bad answers ("redirect them"):
- "So it looks better." → "Better than what? What metric are you measuring that in?"
- "Be on the first page of Google." → "For which keywords? And then what?"
- "We don't know." → Big red flag. Either the client isn't ready yet, or they'll pay you for 3 months of work they'll never evaluate.

### 2. "What's the current setup, if any?"

CMS, hosting, plugins, API integrations, custom code. Ask who ran it before — internal IT, an agency, a freelancer? What were the problems?

The goal: you can estimate how much legacy code you have to understand and how much you're free to rebuild on a new stack.

### 3. "Who's the audience? 1–2 sentences."

Not a persona doc, just 1–2 sentences. If the client can't answer, the project is premature — they need validation first, not a website.

Example of a good answer: "B2B procurement managers in construction, 35–50 years old, looking for a supplier with fast delivery."

From that one sentence you know:
- The site is B2B → conversion via an RFQ form, not instant purchase
- The target group is technical → you can go deep in the specs
- They're looking for "fast delivery" → you have to surface lead times somewhere visible

### 4. "What are the 3 biggest risks?"

The client isn't expecting this one. It forces them to think about what could sink the project.

Common answers:
- "Our copywriter is overloaded." (process problem)
- "GDPR / regulatory requirements." (compliance)
- "Dependence on a single marketing channel." (business risk)
- "The previous agency let us down twice." (trust)

Each of those risks shapes your proposal: who writes the content, how you handle the legal side, how much time you have to spend on communication.

### 5. "What's the budget, and how much flexibility ±?"

The most uncomfortable question. Ask it exactly like that. The client will tell you one of these:

- A specific number ("€8,000, ±20%") → great, you can decide whether to take the project
- A range ("€5–15k") → a 3× spread, the project isn't specified yet — you need to keep discussing or send a proposal with a fixed scope
- "Depends on the offer" → red flag. The client either has no budget, or wants to reverse-engineer your rates by playing 3 freelancers off each other

Don't ask "do you have a budget?". Ask "**what** is your budget?". That way you don't get a yes/no, you get a number.

### 6. "Who decides, who reviews?"

If the client has 4 people in play ("CEO, marketing, IT, legal") with veto power, the project will take twice as long. Ask explicitly:

- Who decides on the final design?
- Who decides on the copy?
- Who approves the budget?

Ideal state: one owner for design/UX, one for content, the CEO for overall direction. Reality: usually one person approves everything (the founder of a small company), which is fast but requires their time.

### 7. "Are there brand guidelines?"

- **Yes, we have a PDF** → great, you get colors, fonts, tone of voice
- **We have a logo and 2 colors** → the minimum, you do the rest yourself (but bill for the brand work)
- **We have nothing, the designer will figure it out** → Careful: they expect it to "get figured out" automatically. You need to spell out that brand identity is a separate scope of work.

### 8. "What are the deal-breakers? (3 minutes)"

The last question. The client names the things that will sink the project. Sometimes total trivialities, sometimes fundamentals.

Examples:
- "We can't use Mailchimp, we're in a legal dispute with them."
- "A headless CMS is out of the question, we need to manage it from a phone."
- "The deadline is Dec 1, no moving it."
- "It can't be on Vercel, we have a contract with AWS."

These 3 minutes save you weeks of later frustration.

## What NOT to do on a discovery call

- **Technical pitching.** "I'd recommend a headless setup with Astro and Sanity…" The client has no idea what that is. You lose 10 minutes and it gets you no closer to a decision. Pick the stack after the call, once you have the info.
- **A pricing commitment.** "This will be €8,000." Without a detailed spec, that's a lottery. Say "after the call I'll send you a proposal with a price range within 24 hours."
- **Your life story.** The client doesn't need to hear about your 8 years of experience. Your portfolio already told them before the call.
- **Sales-y closing.** "So, shall we start next week?" A discovery call isn't a sale. It's a mutual evaluation.

## How to close (the last 3 minutes)

1. **30-second recap**: "So you need X for goal Y, budget Z, deadline W, and the key risk is R. Do I have that right?"
2. **Next step**: "Within 24 hours I'll send you a proposal with a range — from €X to €Y depending on scope. That way you'll have something to compare against."
3. **Boundary check**: "If you have questions or want to clarify something in the meantime, email works. For bigger changes we'll set aside a follow-up call."

## The 24-hour follow-up template

A short email, 5 paragraphs:

```
1. Recap of what I heard (3 sentences)
2. Proposed scope (in scope, out of scope)
3. Price with a range (€X–Y) with a concrete why (what's at the low end, what's at the high end)
4. Timeline (low end vs. high end)
5. Next step: "Pick one of 3 options: A/B/C, or ask a question"
```

No PDF, no pitch deck. A plain-text email or a link to Notion.

## When to turn a project down

After 8 questions you have enough information to decide. Turn it down when:

- The goal is vague ("be modern")
- Budget < hourly rate × estimated hours × 1.5 (buffer)
- The deal-breakers are technically impossible ("you can't use any JavaScript")
- The client has a 4-stage approval process and a 4-week deadline
- The previous 2 freelancers "let them down" — the problem might be the client, not them

It's better to decline right away with "this isn't a good fit for me, I'd recommend my colleague X" than to drag a project out for 6 months.

## TL;DR

8 questions, 30 minutes, no technical pitch, no pricing commitment. Target metric, current setup, audience, risks, budget, decision-making, brand, deal-breakers. A 24-hour follow-up with a proposal in a price range. Decline when the goal is vague or the budget doesn't add up.
