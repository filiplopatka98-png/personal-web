A client called me last month: "Filip, I want AI search on my site, like ChatGPT has." He's got 240 articles and traffic of about 3,000 visits a month. An agency had already tossed him a €4,500 quote for a "RAG solution with OpenAI." I sat down, we ran the numbers, and we ended up with a $99 plugin.

Here are the four real options for small and mid-sized businesses. They differ 100x on price and, for 90% of cases, almost not at all on quality.

## 1. Native WP search + Relevanssi (~€0–120 one-time)

WordPress's default search is dreadful — a `LIKE %query%` across `wp_posts.post_title` and `wp_posts.post_content`, no ranking, no stemming. [Relevanssi](https://www.relevanssi.com/) replaces it with a proper index: TF-IDF ranking, fuzzy matching, synonyms, custom fields.

The free version is enough for most blogs. Premium (€120/year, unlimited sites) adds multi-site indexing and click tracking.

**When:** up to ~500 articles, Slovak/Czech content (Relevanssi handles inflection via stemmers), no extreme latency requirements.

```bash
wp plugin install relevanssi --activate
wp relevanssi index
```

After this one step, 80% of WordPress sites have "AI search" that's better than what they had before. No API keys, no monthly bills, no vendor lock-in. If you're at it, this is also a good moment to look at how many plugins you're carrying — see [going from 28 plugins down to 9](/en/blog/plugin-dieta-z-28-na-9/).

## 2. Algolia (~€30–100/month)

[Algolia](https://www.algolia.com/) is hosted search-as-a-service. Faceted search, instant results, typo tolerance. Pricing on the Grow plan: the first 10,000 searches per month are free, then $0.50 per 1,000 searches (the Grow Plus plan with AI features runs $1.75 per 1,000).

For a store with 5,000 products and 50,000 searches a month, budget roughly €30–70/month, plus integration (there's an official plugin for WP, and `react-instantsearch` for Next.js).

**Pro:** instant search (results under 50 ms on every keystroke), sensible defaults, good analytics.
**Con:** the price climbs fast as you scale, you don't get full control over ranking, and Slovak needs configuration (custom synonyms, language settings).

## 3. Typesense self-hosted (~€4/month on a Hetzner VPS)

[Typesense](https://typesense.org/) is the open-source alternative to Algolia. API-compatible in spirit, REST endpoints, easy docs.

Self-host it on a Hetzner CX22 VPS (€3.79/month) — plenty for 200 articles + 5,000 products. Docker Compose and it's running.

```yaml
services:
  typesense:
    image: typesense/typesense:30.2
    ports: ["8108:8108"]
    volumes: ["./data:/data"]
    command: >
      --data-dir /data
      --api-key=xyz
      --enable-cors
```

You fill the index via the API, and on the client `typesense-instantsearch-adapter` gives you Algolia-style UX. For mid-sized firms with a technical person in-house (or a dev partner), this is the sweet spot — control, price, and performance all at once. It's also what powers the [faceted filters that don't lag — without ElasticSearch](/en/blog/faceted-filtre-bez-elasticsearch/).

## 4. LLM RAG (Claude API + pgvector, €50–500/month)

This is what clients actually mean when they say "AI search." Vector embeddings, semantic similarity, an answer generated by an LLM with citations. Stack: Postgres + pgvector, OpenAI `text-embedding-3-small` ($0.02 per 1M tokens), Claude Sonnet or one of the cheaper models (e.g. Claude Haiku) for re-ranking and generation.

Cost at 3,000 unique queries a month: ~$5 for embeddings + ~$30 for LLM completions + $20 for hosting = ~$55/month. At 30,000 queries it jumps to $300 and up.

**When it makes sense:**
- documentation, a knowledge base, long articles where users ask "how do I do X"
- multilingual content (semantic search ignores language)
- a chatbot-style UX, not just a list of links

**When it doesn't:**
- a store with 200 products (Algolia/Typesense will be faster and more accurate)
- a news site (chronological, keyword-driven)
- any site where the user knows exactly what they're looking for

## Decision tree

| Situation | Solution |
|---|---|
| WordPress, < 500 articles | Relevanssi Premium |
| WordPress, store, < 1,000 SKUs, tight budget | Relevanssi |
| WordPress store, > 5,000 SKUs | Algolia or Typesense |
| Next.js / Astro headless, technical team | Typesense self-hosted |
| Knowledge base / documentation / chatbot UX | LLM RAG (pgvector) |
| Multilingual, semantic queries | LLM RAG |

## Practical takeaways from three projects this year

1. **Store with 1,200 products** — Relevanssi Premium ($99/year), zero monthly cost. Time to implement: 4 hours, including a custom synonym set.
2. **Real-estate site, 380 listings** — Typesense on a CX22, ~€5/mo. Faceted search (location, price, m²) that WordPress never delivered. 12 hours of dev.
3. **B2B SaaS documentation, ~600 pages** — pgvector + Claude. €70/mo for the whole stack. This was the one case where RAG genuinely added value — the docs have a long tail of "how-to" questions.

## TL;DR

Before you pay €4,500 for "AI search," try Relevanssi for a few dozen euros. In 7 out of 10 cases it's all you need. If it isn't, jump straight to Typesense — it has a better price/performance ratio than Algolia for most SK projects. Put RAG off until you have a clear use case above 500 documents and users who ask in full sentences, not keywords.
